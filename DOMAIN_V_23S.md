# Domain V of 23S rRNA

Domain V is the catalytic heart of the large ribosomal subunit (50S in prokaryotes, 60S in eukaryotes). It is the most highly conserved and functionally critical part of the ribosomal RNA (rRNA).

## Peptidyl Transferase Center (PTC)

The most important feature of Domain V is the **Peptidyl Transferase Center (PTC)**. This is the active site where the chemical reaction of peptide bond formation occurs.

*   **Ribozyme Nature:** The catalytic activity of the PTC resides entirely within the RNA of Domain V, confirming that the ribosome is a ribozyme.
*   **Central Loop:** The PTC is formed by a large, highly conserved "central loop" in the secondary structure of Domain V.

## Essential Residues (E. coli numbering)

Several nucleotides within the central loop of Domain V are critical for the binding of tRNAs and the catalysis of the peptidyl transfer reaction.

| Residue | Function / Significance |
| :--- | :--- |
| **G2252** | Involved in tRNA binding in the P-site. |
| **A2451** | One of the most conserved residues; critical for the proximity and orientation of the reactants. |
| **U2506** | Plays a significant role in the environment of the P-site. |
| **U2585** | Highly conserved; interacts with the tRNA and is essential for the catalytic mechanism. |

## Evolutionary Significance: The Accretion Model

According to the **Accretion Model** developed by Loren Williams and colleagues, Domain V is the oldest part of the ribosome.

*   **The Proto-Ribosome:** The core of Domain V, specifically the symmetrical pocket at the PTC, is thought to have evolved from a primitive RNA dimer capable of joining amino acids (see [PROTO_RIBOSOM.md](PROTO_RIBOSOM.md)).
*   **Ribosome Expansion:** Over billions of years, other domains (I, II, III, IV, VI) were added "on top" of this ancient core through a process of accretion, increasing the ribosome's complexity and stability.

## Antibiotic Target

Because of its central role in protein synthesis, Domain V is a primary target for many antibiotics.

*   **Chloramphenicol:** Inhibits peptide bond formation by binding to the PTC in Domain V.
*   **Macrolides:** Bind near the PTC and block the exit tunnel, preventing the elongation of the polypeptide chain.
